## The Core Principle

"The mind is only a bundle of thoughts. The thoughts arise because there is the thinker. The thinker is the ego. If you enquire 'Who am I?' the mind will go back to its source; the thought that arose will become quiescent."
— Ramana Maharshi

All thoughts depend on the fundamental "I"-thought - the sense of being a separate self. By tracing this "I"-thought to its source, all thoughts subside, and what remains is the Self - pure awareness that was always present.

---

## The Method

### Step 1: Notice a Thought

When any thought arises - worry, desire, memory, plan, question - notice it. This is the material for inquiry.

### Step 2: Ask "To Whom Does This Thought Arise?"

Direct this question sincerely. Not as a mantra, but as genuine investigation. The thought appeared - to whom?

### Step 3: The Answer: "To Me"

This answer will come naturally. The thought arose to "me" - to this sense of being an individual.

### Step 4: Ask "Who Am I?"

Now the fundamental question. Not seeking an answer in words, but turning attention toward the source of this "me."

### Step 5: Trace the "I" to Its Source

Follow the sense of "I" inward. Where does it come from? The I-thought arises from the Heart (not the physical heart, but the spiritual center, experienced on the right side of the chest).

### Step 6: Remain as Awareness

When the I-thought is traced to its source, it subsides. What remains is pure awareness - the Self. Rest here. This is not an experience but the recognition of what you always are.

## Key Distinctions

### This Is Not:
- Mechanical repetition of "Who am I?"
- Philosophical analysis of the self-concept
- Creating an answer to the question
- Visualizing or imagining the Self
- A mantra or affirmation

### This Is:
- Genuine inquiry into the nature of the questioner
- Turning attention from objects to the subject
- Following the I-thought to its source
- Discovering what remains when the I-thought subsides
- Recognition, not acquisition

---

## Dealing with Thoughts

Thoughts will arise during practice. This is not failure - each thought is an opportunity for inquiry.

**The approach:**
1. Don't fight the thought
2. Don't suppress the thought
3. Don't follow the thought
4. Instead, trace it to its source

**The mechanism:**
Every thought depends on the I-thought. When you trace any thought back through "To whom?" → "To me" → "Who am I?", you address the root rather than the branches.

**Ramana's teaching:** "Instead of one making his mind to roam outside, retaining it in the Heart and firmly fixing it there, is called atma-vichara (enquiry into the Self)."

---

## The Heart Center

Ramana located the spiritual Heart on the right side of the chest - not the physical heart, not the heart chakra, but the source from which the I-thought arises.

**Practical guidance:**
- Don't force attention to a location
- Natural inquiry leads attention to settle in this region
- The Heart is not a physical place but the source of the sense of "I"
- When the I-thought subsides into the Heart, what remains is the Self

## Common Obstacles

### Obstacle 1: "I can't find the I"
**Response:** You are searching for the I as if it were an object. The one searching IS the I. Turn attention to the searcher.

### Obstacle 2: "My mind is too restless"
**Response:** Each distraction is material for inquiry. Ask "To whom is this distraction?" The very restlessness becomes the path.

### Obstacle 3: "I get answers but nothing happens"
**Response:** You are answering intellectually. The question is not meant to produce an answer but to redirect attention. Stay with the looking, not with any answer found.

### Obstacle 4: "I feel peaceful but not realized"
**Response:** Peace is good but don't stop there. Who feels peaceful? Continue the inquiry. The Self is not a feeling but that which is aware of all feelings.

## Stages of Practice

### Stage 1: Effortful Inquiry
The mind must be repeatedly turned back from thoughts through the inquiry process. This requires vigilance and discipline.

### Stage 2: Natural Inquiry
With practice, the inquiry becomes more natural. The mind develops the habit of turning inward rather than outward.

### Stage 3: Continuous Inquiry
The inquiry becomes nearly constant - a background awareness that traces all activity to its source.

### Stage 4: Sahaja (Natural State)
The inquiry has done its work. The separate self is seen through. What remains is the natural state - ordinary awareness recognized as the Self. No effort is required to maintain this.

## Sample Application

**Seeker says:** "I've tried asking 'Who am I?' but I just get mental answers. My mind says 'I am awareness' or 'I am consciousness' but nothing changes."

**Self-Inquiry Response:**

The mind will always produce answers - that is its nature. But you are not seeking an answer. The question is not for the mind.

Consider: when you ask "Who am I?" and the mind produces "I am awareness," who is aware of that answer? There is something prior to the answer, aware of the answer. That is what you seek.

Do not accept any answer the mind gives. Each answer is another thought. To that thought, ask again: "To whom does this thought 'I am awareness' arise?" Again the answer: "To me." Again the question: "Who am I?"

You are not trying to arrive at a correct answer. You are using the question to turn attention away from thoughts - including the thought "I am awareness" - toward that which is prior to all thoughts.

When all answers are rejected, what remains? There is still awareness - but not as an answer, not as a thought. Just awareness being aware. Not "I am awareness" (a thought) but simple being-awareness without words.

Rest there. Don't name it. Don't claim it. Just be it.

The inquiry is complete not when you find a good answer but when the questioner dissolves. Then there is no one left to ask "Who am I?" and no one to answer. Just this. As it always was.
